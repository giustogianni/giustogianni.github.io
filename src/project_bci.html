Overview

Detection of error-related potentials in EEG recorded signals

Spring 2019

Abstract

Electroencephalography (EEG) provides a non-invasive tool for the recording and monitoring of brain electrical activity. It is often used in brain-computer interaction (BCI) experiments where the goal is to decode the patient intended movement from its brain activity and translate it to a device that can perform motor actions for example. Most techniques in this field rely on the detection of so-called event-related potentials (ERPs) (or evoked potentials) which arise in response to external stimuli.

In the current project, we focus on a particular type of ERPs, namely the error-related potentials (ErrPs). They are generated when incorrect actions are observed by the subject or more precisely when a mismatch between the expected output and the actual action occurs. ErrPs are observed in EEG signals as a waveform characterized by a steep deflection toward negative voltage values (referred as N200) immediately followed be an increase in the voltage (referred as P300).

We use a simple experimental design in order to evoke ErrPs in 5 subjects. The latter are asked to direct a moving cursor toward a fixed target with the help of a joystick while an erroneous deviation is intentionally introduced in some trials. ErrPs are then detected offline from data originating from EEG recording after few processing steps. The final resulting signals are finally used for linear discriminant analysis with the help of PCA. In addition, we also implement a convolutional neural network (CNN) for this classification task.
